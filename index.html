Step 1

A one-degree change can sound small.

Climate change is not only about the average number. It is also about how often people cross uncomfortable or dangerous heat thresholds.

Step 2

We start from 2020.

This project uses 2020 as a baseline. Every future value is compared with this starting point, so we can see how each place changes over time.

Step 3

Average warming is meaningful.

By 2070, average temperature changes across many states. Even gradual warming matters because it shifts the climate people and ecosystems are used to.

Step 4

The future depends on emissions.

Low, medium, and high emissions do not lead to the same map. By mid-century, higher emissions can push more places into stronger warming.

Step 5

But averages hide the days people feel.

A yearly average smooths over extreme days. When we count days above 35°C, the future becomes more personal.

Step 6

Extreme heat does not rise evenly.

Some states gain many more 35°C+ days than others. Similar average warming can still lead to very different lived heat exposure.

Step 7

High emissions make the heat story sharper.

Under higher emissions, the change is not only a warmer average. It can also mean more days when heat crosses a threshold.

Takeaway

Average warming helps us see the long-term climate shift. Extreme hot days help us understand how that shift may become part of daily life.
