01 · If

Average warming can look small and abstract.

The grey line shows observed U.S. average temperature from 2000 to 2020, and the colored lines show CMIP6 futures through 2100. On a chart, a few degrees can look like a slow, abstract shift.

02 · But

Averages hide threshold-crossing days.

People do not experience a yearly average directly. Heat is felt when daily maximum temperatures cross dangerous thresholds, so we translate warming into additional summer days above 35°C.

03 · But

Extra 35°C+ days do not land evenly across states.

Under high emissions, compare your hometown state with the state projected to gain the most baseline-aligned 5-year average summer 35°C+ days by 2100.

04 · Therefore

Track baseline-aligned additional summer 35°C+ days.

A fixed 35°C threshold turns small shifts in daily maximum temperature into more threshold-crossing days. That is why we track additional summer 35°C+ days directly after aligning CMIP6 futures to the observed 2020 baseline.

05 · But

Hazard alone is not exposure.

We now animate the map in consistent 10-year steps from observed history into the 2100 projection. Bubbles stay anchored inside states and grow where hot-day hazard and exposed population combine into larger exposure-days.

06 · Therefore

Carry your selected state into exposure.

For your selected state, exposure-days are built from added 35°C+ days and projected population. The view compares your state with the highest-exposure benchmark; if your state is already the highest, it stands alone as the benchmark.

07 · U.S. comparison

By 2100, U.S. heat exposure becomes much larger.

Each block represents the same amount of heat exposure. One exposure-day means one person experiencing one additional 35°C+ summer day.

08 · Context

Some groups and systems may face different pressure.

Older adults, cooling demand, humidity, hot-dry risk, and crops help interpret why extra 35°C+ days matter. These are proxies and context, not direct predictions of health outcomes, electricity bills, drought severity, or crop yield.

Takeaway

Average warming delta describes the climate shift. Summer 35°C+ day delta describes the days people live through.

A small average-temperature delta can still mean many more days above an extreme heat threshold. That is where climate change becomes daily risk.
